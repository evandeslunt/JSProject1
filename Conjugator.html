Verb Agreement and Conjugation

In many languages, verbs must agree with their subject. In English, we add an -s to verbs when the subject is third person singular:

Third Person Singular First Person Singular Third Person Plural
He walks I walk They walk
She says I say They say

In other languages, such as Spanish, the rules for verb agreement are more complex. Spanish verbs must be changed so they agree with their subject and the tense. This is called "conjugation."

To conjugate a Spanish verb, you must first look at the last two letters of the verb ("ar", "er", or "ir"), then replace those letters with the suffix that agrees with the subject and tense. The three tables below show the suffixes for each type of verb in the present tense.

Spanish Verb Suffixes (Present Tense)

Spanish Pronoun English Pronoun -ar Suffix -er Suffix -ir Suffix
Yo I -o -o -o
Tu You -as -es -es
El/Ella/Usted He/She/You -a -e -e
Vosotros* You -ás -és -ís
Nosotros We -amos -emos -imos
Ellos/Ellas/Ustedes They/You -an -en -en

*Vosotros is primarily used in Spain. Most dialects of South American Spanish do not use Vosotros, so it is usually left out of conjugation tables created for people learning South American Spanish.

Of course, as with every rule, there are exceptions! "Irregular" verbs do not follow these patterns. But most Spanish verbs are regular, or very close to regular. Click the next tab to practice conjugating some regular verbs!

Examples

Here are some regular verbs to conjugate:

  • hablar ("talk")
  • caminar ("walk")
  • escribir ("write")

  • beber ("drink")

Conclusion

If you want to speak with proper grammar, it's important to conjugate your verbs correctly. English verb conjugation is easy (just add -s to third person singular in the present tense) but in other languages, conjugation is more complicated.

We've only looked at Spanish regular verbs in the present tense. There are more rules for irregular verbs, semi-irregular verbs, and verbs in other tenses.
